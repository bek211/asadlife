About Me

My name is Asadbek Eshkobilov, a resilient, multi-talented individual born in 2001 in rural Uzbekistan. My life story is not just a sequence of events, but a testament to hard work, adaptability, and relentless commitment to growth, even when opportunities were scarce.

My Complete Story

🏔️ My Family Background

I come from a large family living in a remote mountain village called Qoraobdol, in Forish district of Jizzakh region in Uzbekistan. There are seven of us in total — my parents, three sons, and two daughters. I am the eldest child, and with that comes responsibility.

We were never a wealthy family — not by modern standards. Yes, we keep goats, cows, and chickens, and we have vineyards and fruit trees. But that's not a sign of wealth in the village — it's a way of survival and self-sufficiency. These animals aren't luxury; they're part of our daily routine, part of the life we were born into.

We live far from the city. In fact, during dry years, we couldn't find water anywhere and had to dig our own well as a family. In our rural life, everything depends on physical work. We don't have a large income or modern conveniences. We don't own expensive technology and we don't go on vacations. Instead, we work — every season, every day. We grow food, take care of animals, and help each other.

Our life was simple, sometimes difficult, but it taught me values that can't be bought — discipline, teamwork, humility, and gratitude. While we're not financially wealthy, we're rich in experience, effort, and family support. And this kind of wealth shaped who I am today.

I completed my secondary education between 2008 and 2019, achieving excellent grades in all subjects and numerous awards that recognized both my academic success and my contributions to school and community life. I was honored as "Best Student of the Year," achieved top positions in district legal olympiads, and actively participated in team intellectual competitions at school. My school years were marked by a hunger for knowledge and a deep interest in history.

From 2012 to 2019, I was consistently an excellent student. My teachers truly knew my potential, and sometimes I think I needed a school in the city with more opportunities to face real competition and reach my full academic potential.

This passion led me to study at the National University of Uzbekistan, one of the world's top 1000 universities, where I graduated in 2024 with a bachelor's degree in history. But learning for me was never confined to the classroom. From a young age, I immersed myself in practical work that shaped my character and expanded my skills.

I come from a 7-member family - my parents, 3 sons and 2 daughters, with me being the eldest child. We are self-employed as family farmers and livestock keepers in our village. I worked extensively in agriculture: harvesting potatoes and fruits with care, tending to vineyards. For watermelon and melon work, I traveled several times to neighboring districts to help relatives during harvest, as these crops don't grow in our village due to lack of rivers. I spent many seasons working in fields, adapting to extreme weather conditions while maintaining high productivity.

Additionally, I worked in a textile workshop as a computer embroidery machine operator, learning to handle complex machinery and maintain high precision under pressure. I also worked in supermarkets, where I was responsible for warehouse operations, unloading goods, arranging products on store shelves, and maintaining organized inventory.

In 2024, I joined Yandex as a courier, where I managed time-sensitive deliveries and developed better navigation and communication skills. These jobs weren't glamorous, but they gave me resilience and deep respect for all forms of work.

🏠 Complete House Reconstruction - Unforgettable Year

A transformational year when our Soviet-era family house from the 70s became uninhabitable after more than 40 years. The foundation had collapsed, walls absorbed moisture, the roof constantly leaked. We decided: tear everything down and rebuild from the ground up.

🔨 Complete Demolition and Reconstruction Process

• Active demolition leadership: As the eldest son, I led every phase from roof dismantling to brick wall demolition with family and neighbors
• Foundation work: Participated in site clearing, leveling, and concrete foundation pouring with skilled workers
• Traditional building methods: We used local materials - clay, straw, mud, bricks, reeds instead of modern urban materials like steel reinforcement
• Practical masonry: Learned proper brick laying, clay mortar preparation, wall leveling, measuring, leveling, and corner setting

🪵 Roof Construction and Traditional Methods

• Wooden frame assembly: Measured, cut, and joined wooden beams to create the main roof structure
• Traditional roofing: Nailed wide wooden planks, laid reed bundles, applied two layers of clay mixture
• Timeline: Roof construction started in September, completed in 15-20 days using traditional village methods
• Gradual completion: Initial 3 rooms completed in 2018, remaining rooms renovated in 2019, 2023, 2024 (ongoing)

💡 Electrical and Technical Skills Development

• Home electrical systems 220V: Learned installation of outlets, switches, lights, and circuit breakers
• Independent learning: Worked with electricians, then started doing installations independently
• TV antenna setup: Mastered antenna installation and cable connections
• Community service: Helped neighbors with their electrical installations

🛠 Complete Set of Mastered Skills

2014-2025

🌾 Extensive Practical Experience from Early Adolescence

Traditional rural life, real work — not a hobby, a way of life

I grew up in a remote village about 100 km from the nearest city, I didn't spend my childhood visiting zoos or museums. My zoo was the livestock pen. My "museum" was the walnut tree — 12 meters tall and I had to climb it like an acrobat. 🎪 While other kids fed pigeons in the park, I fed sheep and goats — rain, snow, or scorching heat, it didn't matter. ☀️❄️🌧️ Responsibility doesn't take a vacation when you grow up in a family like mine.

From age 13 (maybe earlier, but that's as far back as I clearly remember) I started helping the family with all kinds of tasks. It wasn't a one-man show — it was family work, but because I was the eldest child, I carried the heavier burden. 💪 We weren't farmers in a commercial sense — we were traditional family caretakers, practicing ancient ways of animal husbandry and agriculture just for survival and rootedness. 🌱

🐑 Animal Husbandry Experience — Family-led, Survival-driven

  • • Daily care of sheep, goats, and cows — regardless of season or weather 🌦️
  • • Grazing animals in wide fields under sun, wind, rain, and snow ❄️☀️
  • • Cleaning barns and sheds, collecting and preparing feed and hay 🏚️
  • • Shearing wool from sheep and goats during seasonal cycles ✂️
  • • Bathing animals in summer for disease and tick prevention 🛁
  • • Climbing tall trees (10–12 meters high!) for nut and fruit harvest 🌳

🐐 Mixed herds: Goats and sheep grazed together — but don't be fooled, goats are a completely different story. They're energetic, mischievous, and curious about everything that moves (or doesn't move). It's like herding comedians on four legs. 😄 We raised our animals not as part of a farming business, but as a way of life inherited from our ancestors. Goats especially required patience — they're hyperactive, loud, and smart enough to test your nerves daily. 🤯 Sheep were calmer, but keeping them healthy during harsh Uzbek winters meant constant attention and thick skin. 🧤

🌾 Family Farming and Vineyard Work — The Hands That Feed Us

This wasn't a summer internship. This was daily life. And while some kids went to zoos to look at animals, I was out there responsible for them. Not observing from a distance, but up close and personal — shearing their wool, feeding them before I fed myself, and making sure they were safe and healthy. 🦆🐑

🏛️ Cultural Context

In Uzbek villages, this kind of life isn't called child labor — it's called growing up. 👦➡️👨 Boys learn hard work by doing, not by theory. Our elders don't serve us life on a plate; they hand us a shovel, a rope, or a bundle of hay and say, "Let's go." 🛠️ This is how we become men: through sweat, responsibility, and real-life experience.

And me? I wasn't just helping — I was learning. I was building the kind of resilience no school can teach. 💎✨
